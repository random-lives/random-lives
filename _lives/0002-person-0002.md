They called her Little One until she could take a name.

She was born in the deep cold, when snow lay under the oaks and the river ran slow beneath a lid of ice. Their house stood with other houses on higher ground above the wet places, timber and clay sealed against wind. A long fire burned along the floor. Her grandparents lived there, her parents, and an older sister with her husband and child. Work and food were shared, and so were the nights when a baby kept everyone awake.

She was the sixth daughter. Two sisters had already died in infancy. The women in the house kept the younger children close while they worked. When the soil loosened and the first field tasks began, her mother carried her out and back through the day: clearing old stalks, hoeing, and pressing seed into the dark earth. At the edge of the settlement they set aside a little grain and a little milk at a marked post, the offering made the same way each season.

Her father spent most days with the animals. He drove cattle and pigs into the clearings and the edges of the stubble, kept them moving so they did not foul the ground near the houses, and watched the tree line for wolves and for people coming up from the open country to the south.
