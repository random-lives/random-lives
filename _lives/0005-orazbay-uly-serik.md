Orazbay uly Serik was born in the autumn of 1884 on the north slope of the Tianshan, in the grazing country and scattered fields west of Ürümqi, where Qing officials and local headmen both mattered and where Kazakh camps met market towns. His father Serik lived by sheep and horses, moving with seasons and bargaining with settled neighbors; his mother Nurgul turned milk into sour curds, stitched felt, and kept the cooking fire going through wind and cold.

He was the second child in a crowded joint household. His oldest sister Asylzat came first, then Orazbay, then six more children after him. The household included other close kin, sharing a cooking pot and a corral. When elders talked at night, Orazbay listened and asked questions until someone sent him outside to check the animals. He liked being sent on errands because it meant he could talk to other camps. He learned to read and write simple words in the Arabic script from an older relative who kept a small bundle of papers wrapped in cloth. The lessons stayed practical: names, numbers, a few lines of prayer, and short notes for trade.

The winters stayed in his memory. In 1894 his baby brother died at two, after a sickness that spread through several tents. Adults spoke quietly about who had visited whom, who had shared bowls, and what they should burn or wash. Orazbay watched his mother scrub cooking pots with sand and boiling water. After that, he kept a habit of rinsing his own wooden cup carefully, even when other boys laughed at him.

By his early teens he rode out with men for longer stretches and began to spend time near the market routes. He picked up spoken Chinese used by traders and guards—enough to ask prices, answer questions, and keep from being cheated on measures. He took pride in that skill. He also liked a bowl of hot wheat noodles when he had coin, eaten standing by a stall so he could keep watching the crowd.

Asylzat died in 1906 at twenty-four. There was no long explanation in the household; she was there, then she was not. That spring, Serik expected more from his son, and Orazbay accepted it without arguing. The same year, Orazbay married Ainagul. She came into the household with a small dowry bundle, strong hands, and a steady way of speaking. Their first son arrived the next year, and Orazbay began to take hired work more often—herding for others, hauling loads, and doing carting when someone had a wagon and needed a driver.

He moved within northern Xinjiang for work, staying in the orbit of the Ürümqi–Changji corridor and the grazing lands near Hutubi but shifting camps and quarters as jobs changed. He enjoyed the social part of it. He made friends quickly and remembered faces. One was a man named Boranbay, another hired hand, who told crude jokes and played simple dice games with sheep knucklebones when they had time. Orazbay laughed loud and long, then put the bones away first when a foreman approached.

Wage work brought him to places where Russians and other foreigners were visible in small numbers, tied to trade and to the northern border. In 1910 he buried his younger brother Tolegen, who died at twenty-two. The loss tightened the labor pool in the wider kin group. After that, Orazbay spent more nights away from his home camp.

In 1910 his daughter was born, and in 1912 a second son followed. Politics changed overhead. In 1911 the Qing fell, and by the next year the new Republic’s authority in Xinjiang came through new paperwork, new seals, and the same armed men. Taxes and travel permissions shifted. Some days a road guard wanted a note; other days he wanted a fee. Orazbay handled these encounters by talking steadily, offering tea when he had it, and keeping his temper in check until he was back among his own.

In 1909 he began going, quietly at first, to a Russian-speaking Orthodox prayer circle near a market settlement. It met in a room that smelled of wax and damp wood. A lay leader everyone called Father Mikhail read from a book and showed small painted icons. Orazbay stopped attending mosque gatherings and stopped keeping Ramadan. He learned the sign of the cross and accepted a Christian name—Andrei—for use with those men, especially when he needed introductions or help finding work. At home he kept the change mostly to himself and to Ainagul. When asked directly, he answered in short sentences and changed the subject to animals, weather, or prices.

In 1915 Ainagul delivered a daughter who died the same day. Two years later Orazbay lost his sister, the one born in 1890, at twenty-seven. He did not argue about ritual details at those funerals. He focused on practical tasks: digging, carrying water, feeding visitors. That approach kept disputes from attaching to his household.

A fight finally found him in 1919. At a herding camp where he had been promised wages, the pay did not come. He argued with the man responsible, voices rising in front of others. When Boranbay tried to pull him back, Orazbay shook him off, grabbed a whip handle, and struck another hired hand who jeered. The brawl stayed short. Older men stepped in, separated them, and forced apologies. Orazbay paid a small compensation in livestock value and returned to work with a stiff jaw and a bruised hand.

The sharpest loss came in 1923 when his eldest son died at sixteen. The boy had begun to ride out with men and talk about earning his own money, then he was buried before the summer ended. After that, Orazbay accepted militia duty when asked. Under Yang Zengxin’s rule in Xinjiang, the government prized order and kept a close eye on roads. From 1924 through 1928 Orazbay served part-time in a local detachment, standing night guard and escorting requisitions of grain and livestock. A Chinese-speaking contact, Zhang Shouyi, handled lists and stamps; Orazbay could read some of the numbers and kept his own notes in Arabic script. He liked the feeling of being trusted with other people’s goods, and he liked the shared tea on cold nights when guards huddled close.

The 1930s brought harder instability. Armed factions and bandits moved through the countryside as conflicts spread from the east and then rippled across northern Xinjiang. In 1932, after rumors of fighting and seizures, Orazbay’s household packed what they could carry—blankets, sacks of flour, a few tools—and fled to relatives’ winter quarters. Ainagul managed the bundles; Orazbay kept counting heads, checking ropes, and looking back at the track. Weeks later they returned to find corrals damaged and stored goods partly looted.

In 1933, on a trip to a weekly bazaar to look for carting work, Orazbay was stopped by armed men who accused him of carrying messages for a rival group. They beat him in the open, took his belt knife, and stripped him of part of his pay. He went home with split lip and bruises, angry but careful about whom he blamed. After that he avoided certain roads and insisted on traveling with others, even when it meant losing a day’s wages.

Caregiving filled the next years. A close co-resident relative—an older kin member in the same cooking household—suffered sudden paralysis in 1934 and could not get up again. Orazbay hauled fuel, arranged help from neighbors, and set up a low sleeping platform near the fire. Ainagul handled washing and feeding; Orazbay managed the outside work and the visitors. Their youngest daughter, born in 1917, carried water and ran messages. Their adult son Mukhtar, born in 1912, began to take on more decisions about work and animals.

A severe winter fever struck Orazbay in early 1936. When it passed, something in him stayed altered. He lost track of simple sequences. He asked the same question twice within minutes. He put a kettle on and forgot it. Once he walked out to fetch a tool and returned without it, insisting it had been stolen. Ainagul stopped letting him travel alone. Mukhtar or a son-in-law came with him even on short trips, and sometimes they kept him home entirely, giving him small tasks like twisting rope or sorting wool where someone could watch.

By the early 1940s the household was openly three generations under one roof: Orazbay and Ainagul, Mukhtar with his wife and children, and other kin passing through. Trade tightened under wartime pressure and border controls; cloth and metal tools cost more, and tea came irregularly. Orazbay still enjoyed hot tea and bread when it was available. He sat near the doorway in late afternoon, listening for familiar voices, and became restless when strangers spoke too fast.

On March 12, 1943, he collapsed with a stroke and died the same day. His body was washed and wrapped, then placed in the ground near the local burial place; men recited prayers over him while his household set out bread and tea for those who came to pay respects.
